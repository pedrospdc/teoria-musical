###  Subsection 14.4.1 The “1 (2) &” Ritmo

In this section, we will discuss some accompanimental rhythms that occur frequently in popular music. The first such ritmo has two acordes per compasso, with the first acorde on beat 1 and the second acorde on the upbeat after beat 2. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-marvin-gaye.svg)

Figura 14.4.1. Marvin Gaye and Ed Townsend, “Let’s Get It On” (1973)

You will find a similar ritmo and bass line in the next example. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-sheeran.svg)

Figura 14.4.2. Ed Sheeran, “Thinking Out Loud” (2014)

The “1 (2) &” ritmo is also found in “Don’t You (Forget About Me),” a song associated with the film The Breakfast Club. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-dont-you.svg)

Figura 14.4.3. Keith Forsey and Steve Schiff, “Don’t You (Forget About Me)” (1985)

Below are five more examples of block acorde accompaniment in the “1 (2) &” ritmo. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-heart-never.svg)

Figura 14.4.4. Holly Knight, Gene Bloch, Ann Wilson, and Nancy Wilson, “Never” (1985)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-kesha.svg)

Figura 14.4.5. Kesha Sebert, Lukasz Gottwald, Benny Blanco, “Tik Tok” (2009)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-bruno-mars.svg)

Figura 14.4.6. Bruno Mars, Philip Lawrence, Christopher Brown, James Fauntleroy, Johnathan Yip, Ray Romulus, Jeremy Reeves, Ray McCullough II, “That’s What I Like” (2017)

Notice that the example above (“That’s What I Like”) has the same progressão as [“September”](ShorterProgressionsFromTheCircleOfFifths.html#september "Figura 9.3.12") by Earth, Wind, and Fire. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-see-you-again.svg)

Figura 14.4.7. Charlie Puth, Cameron Thomaz, Andrew Cedar, Justin Franks, Dann Hume, Josh Hardy, and Phoebe Cockburn, “See You Again” (2015)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-say-you-wont-let-go.svg)

Figura 14.4.8. James Arthur, Neil Ormandy, Steve Solomon, “Say You Won’t Let Go” (2016)

The example below has the “1 (2) &” ritmo in diminution. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-12and-bieber.svg)

Figura 14.4.9. Justin Bieber, Benny Blanco, and Ed Sheeran, “Love Yourself” (2015)

###  Subsection 14.4.2 The “Barbara Ann” Ritmo

It is easier to describe the next block acorde accompaniment padrão as the “Barbara Ann” ritmo than the “1 2 3 (4) & (1) & (2) & 3” ritmo. Below are six examples of pieces that use this ritmo, whether with block acordes or solely in the bass line. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-walking-barbara-ann.svg)

Figura 14.4.10. Fred Fassert, “Barbara Ann” (1961)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-walking-cant-hurry-love.svg)

Figura 14.4.11. Brian Holland, Lamont Dozier, Eddie Holland, “You Can’t Hurry Love” (1966)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-walking-maneater.svg)

Figura 14.4.12. Sara Allen, Daryl Hall, and John Oates, “Maneater” (1982)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-walking-on-sunshine.svg)

Figura 14.4.13. Kimberley Rew, “Walking On Sunshine” (1985)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-walking-jet.svg)

Figura 14.4.14. Nic Cester and Cameron Muncey, “Are You Gonna Be My Girl” (2003)

A slower version of this ritmo occurs in Deep Purple’s “Smoke on the Water.” 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-walking-deep-purple.svg)

Figura 14.4.15. Ritchie Blackmore, Ian Gillan, Roger Glover, Jon Lord, and Ian Paice, “Smoke on the Water” (1973)

###  Subsection 14.4.3 Repeated 8th-nota Acordes

Repeated 8th-nota acordes occur regularly in “classical” music textures. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-8ths-k310.svg)

Figura 14.4.16. W.A. Mozart, Piano Sonata K. 310, I (1778)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-8ths-gute-nacht.svg)

Figura 14.4.17. Franz Schubert, Winterreise, D. 911“Gute Nacht” (1828)

![](external/images/unit3/texture-8ths-ich-grolle.svg)

Figura 14.4.18. Robert Schumann, Dichterliebe, “Ich grolle nicht” (1844)

The next example is from the musical Annie. Nota the “1 (2) &” ritmo in the bass line. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-8ths-hard-knock.svg)

Figura 14.4.19. Charles Strouse and Martin Charnin, Annie, “It’s the Hard Knock Life” (1977)

Repeated eighth notas are a standard accompanimental texture in rock. 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-8ths-heartbreaker.svg)

Figura 14.4.20. Geoff Gill and Cliff Wade, “Heartbreaker” (1979)

The repeated eighth-nota ritmo is also common in recent popular music. Nota that the following example uses the 1950’s progressão (i.e., \\(\left.\text{I}\right.\\)–\\(\left.\text{vi}\right.\\)–\\(\left.\text{IV}\right.\\)–\\(\left.\text{V}\right.\\)). 

![](external/images/unit3/texture-8ths-we-are-young.svg)

Figura 14.4.21. Nate Ruess, Andrew Dost, Jack Antonoff, and Jeff Bhasker, “We Are Young” (2011)
